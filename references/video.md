# Video guide (founder video and demo video)

This file covers the two kinds of video that commonly appear in startup program applications: the founder (or team) video, and the demo video. Each has a different job. Do not confuse them.

**Follow the specific program's instructions first.** If the program says the founder video must be 2 minutes, make it 2 minutes, even if this guide says 1 minute. If the program asks for a demo video with voiceover, include voiceover. Program instructions always override the general guidance in this file. Not following directions is one of the ways programs screen out applicants.

---

## Founder / team video

### What it is for

The founder video is where the program sees how the founders communicate. It is not a product demo. It is not a sales pitch. It is a short introduction to the humans behind the company.

Programs watch this video to answer questions like: Do these founders come across as credible? Can they explain what they are doing clearly? Do they seem like people I would want to work with for the next three months?

### The gold standard instructions (from YC)

YC's application instructions for the founder video are the clearest in the industry. Use them as a default for any founder video unless the specific program says otherwise.

From YC's application:

- "In the video please introduce yourselves, explain what you're doing and why, and tell us anything else you want to about the founders or the project."
- "The video should be 1 minute long and should contain nothing except the founders talking."
- "This is not the place to submit a demo or promotional video. If you have a demo for your product, there is a separate place in the application for that. For this video, we want to hear how the founders communicate."
- "If you have more than one founder, have all of your founders in the video. If you can't be in the same room at the same time, screen record a video call instead."
- "Do not recite a written script: Use bullet points instead. Using bullet points, just talk spontaneously as you would to a friend. Reading a written script doesn't help your application or convey communication skills."

### What this means in practice

**Keep it to the founders.** No b-roll, no product footage, no background music, no graphics. Just the founders on camera, talking.

**Do not script it.** This is the single biggest mistake. A scripted founder video sounds robotic, and programs can tell instantly. Bullet points are enough. Talk through them like you are explaining the company to a friend at a coffee shop.

**All founders on camera.** If you have co-founders, they should all appear. If you cannot be in the same room, use a screen-recorded video call. Do not submit a video with only one founder if there are multiple on the team.

**Length matters.** If the program says 1 minute, do not submit 2 minutes. Respecting the limit is a signal of discipline and attention to directions.

**Setup can be simple.** A phone camera in landscape mode, decent lighting, a quiet room, no wind or echo. That is enough. You do not need a studio.

### Common mistakes to avoid

- Reading from a script (the biggest one)
- Over-producing the video with music, text overlays, or cuts
- Making it longer than requested
- Only showing one founder when there are multiple
- Showing the product instead of the founders
- Making it feel like a TV commercial or a Shark Tank pitch
- Spending more time on setup than on the content

## Demo video

### What it is for

The demo video shows how the product solves a real user's problem. It is not a feature tour. It is not a marketing video. It is a short, clear demonstration of the product in action.

Programs watch this to answer questions like: Does the product actually work? Does it solve a real problem? Can the founders explain the value without hiding behind buzzwords?

### The core principle

**Cover use cases, not features.** This is the most important rule. A feature tour shows what the product does. A use case shows why it matters.

The right structure for a demo video is:

1. Here is a user (be specific: who they are, what their role is, what their context is).
2. Here is the problem they have.
3. Here is how our product solves it.

Then, if you have time and the product serves multiple user types:

4. Here is a different user with a different problem.
5. Here is how the product solves that one too.

Two or three use cases is usually enough for a demo video. Focus on the main value propositions, not every feature.

### Example of the difference

**Feature tour (weak):**

> "This is our dashboard. You can filter by date, you can export to CSV, you can share with your team. Over here is the settings panel, where you can configure..."

This tells the viewer nothing about why anyone would use the product.

**Use case (strong):**

> "Maria is a hiring manager at a 50-person company. She gets 200 applications per role and spends 10 hours a week sorting through them. Here is how our product helps her. She uploads the job description, and within 30 seconds she has a ranked shortlist of the top 10 candidates. That 10-hour task becomes a 15-minute review."

The viewer now understands who the product is for, what the problem is, and why the product matters.

### What this means in practice

**Keep it simple.** A screen recording is enough. You do not need animations, fancy transitions, or a voiceover actor. Your own voice, explaining what is on the screen, works fine.

**Use plain language.** No buzzwords, no product jargon. Describe what you are doing in the same way you would describe it to a friend who does not know your industry.

**Show, do not tell.** Let the product do the talking. If you need to explain every screen with a voiceover because the product is not self-explanatory, that is itself a signal worth addressing.

**Length varies by program.** Check the program's instructions. Demo videos are often 2-3 minutes, but some programs ask for shorter (1 minute) or longer (5 minutes). Follow their rules.

**Prepare the environment.** Clean up your screen. Close irrelevant tabs. Use sample data that is realistic but not sensitive. If the product has logins or personal data, blur or mask it.

### Common mistakes to avoid

- Doing a feature tour instead of covering use cases
- Over-producing with background music, animations, or a professional voiceover
- Explaining every screen in detail (the viewer does not need a full product manual)
- Using jargon or internal product names that a reader would not understand
- Making it too long
- Showing real customer data or credentials
- Not rehearsing, so the video is full of "um" and long pauses
- Rehearsing so much that it sounds robotic (find the balance)

---

## A shared rule for both video types

**Always follow the specific program's instructions.** Every program has its own rules about length, format, content, and file type. Read the instructions carefully. If they contradict anything in this guide, follow the program.

Not following video instructions is one of the most common reasons strong applications get passed over. Programs watch for it deliberately.
