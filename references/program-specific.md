# Program-specific notes

The question-by-question guide covers what is common across programs. This file covers what is specific to YC and Techstars.

Only reference a section when the founder has named that specific program or asked about it.

---

## Y Combinator

### How YC reads applications

A partner reading your application has 100 applications to get through that day. They have already read 50 before yours. They have 50 to go after. Yours has to be understandable and interesting in the first 20 seconds or it gets passed over.

The order they read matters:

1. **First ~20 seconds: the idea.** What is your company going to make? If they cannot understand the idea quickly, they may not keep reading.
2. **Next pass: the founders.** Who are you? What kind of group is this? What is the most impressive thing each of you has done?
3. **If both look promising: insight.** What is the specific, non-obvious insight that makes your idea work?
4. **If founders are promising but idea is not: look at the "other ideas" question.** Many groups get funded to work on ideas they listed as alternates.

### What YC specifically weighs

**Technical talent on the founding team.** The single biggest variable in whether you get an interview. Teams where at least one founder could be hired into a technical role at a top YC company have 5x better odds than teams without. Biotech and hard tech count. Internships at top companies (Stripe, Airbnb, etc.) count.

If you do not have technical talent, use YC's co-founder matching. This is a real path in; multiple founders in recent batches met through it.

**Teams working on unlaunched "tarpit ideas" without technical founders have the lowest odds.** That is a fact. If this is you, either add technical talent, launch something to generate evidence of demand, or both.

**Evidence of seriousness.** Have founders left their jobs? Have they applied multiple times with more progress each time? Are they using their own product? These signals tell YC you are committed, not dabbling.

**Insight, not execution promises.** "We will build a better UI" is not an insight. "The reason existing products are bad is X, and we are doing Y because of it" is an insight.

### Specific YC questions and patterns

**"What is your company going to make?"**

This is the first question YC reads. Make it count. The matter-of-fact rule is strongest here. No marketing speak. Lead with a concrete description. Use the "variant of something known" trick if it helps.

**"Tell us in one or two sentences about something impressive that each founder has built or achieved."**

This is the most important question on the application. Open-ended by design. Magnitude matters more than category. A specific, concrete example beats a list of generic strengths. Do not list the current startup as the impressive achievement.

**"Tell us about a time you most successfully hacked a non-computer system to your advantage."**

The wildcard question. YC has invited people to interviews primarily on the strength of this answer. Take it seriously. Tell a specific, concrete story.

**Disclose flaws in your idea.** If YC can see obstacles you did not mention, they assume you have not thought of them. Disclosing flaws actually strengthens the application because it shows self-awareness. YC cares more about the founders than the idea.

### The founder video

Submit one. Statistically, teams with videos are much more likely to be invited to interviews. Follow the instructions YC provides. Attention to directions is itself a signal.

### The YC interview

If invited, the interview is a 10-minute Zoom call. Two to four YC partners on their side. Assume they have your application open.

**What YC is looking for:**
- Can you answer basic questions about your business well?
- Do you understand your own numbers?
- Do you know what to work on next?
- Are you credible, honest, and self-aware?
- Are you someone YC would enjoy working with for 3 months?

**What kills interviews:**
- Treating it as adversarial. The partners on the call are the ones you will work with if accepted. Being weird, mean, or combative backfires badly.
- Memorised speeches. YC hates these.
- Not listening to the question asked. Answering the question you prepared for instead of the one you were asked.
- Stretching the truth. If they sense you are trying to fool them, the trust is gone.

**What works:**
- Be yourself. Not Shark Tank yourself. Actual yourself.
- Listen to the question. Answer it directly.
- Show that you know your business deeply.
- Be honest about challenges. Confidence and honesty are not in conflict.

### After an interview rejection

YC sends feedback with each interview rejection. This feedback is specific and actionable. Founders who re-apply having addressed the feedback are heavily weighted in their favour. Many accepted YC founders applied 3, 5, or 7 times, each time addressing the prior feedback.

Internalising and addressing that feedback in a future application genuinely helps. This is tracked.

### YC traps to avoid

- **Saying "we are too early".** No such thing. Many YC companies pivoted after getting in.
- **Saying "we are too far along".** YC funds companies with $1M+ raised, $500K+ ARR, and companies with no code written.
- **Trusting people who claim they can help you get into YC.** They are scammers. YC decisions are made by reading forms. No warm intro or insider connection helps.
- **Listening to people who discourage you from applying.** Ask them if they will invest themselves. If not, their opinion is cheap.
- **Stretching the truth about revenue or traction.** Saying monthly revenue as if it is annual. This is automatic disqualification.

## Techstars

### How Techstars applications differ from YC

Techstars applications ask more structured questions with longer, more explicit prompts. Where YC leaves room for creative interpretation, Techstars often tells you exactly what they want ("here is what to include in your competitor answer: direct, indirect, and potential future competitors").

Follow the structure Techstars asks for. If they ask for three categories of competitor, give them three categories.

### Specific Techstars emphases

**Bottom-up TAM is required.** Techstars specifically asks "how much money could you make?" and expects a bottom-up calculation: ACV × number of potential customers. Top-down market size claims ("the global X market is $Y billion") will not satisfy the question.

**Target audience: be specific about the decision-maker.** Techstars wants to see the persona who has the problem, knows they have it, has quantified it, and will do something about it. "Fitness centers" is wrong. "The 60 COOs of YMCA Metro Groups who control 1,000 facilities" is right.

**Customer acquisition: what can you do better, cheaper, or faster?** Techstars specifically asks what you can do in acquisition that a well-funded competitor could not replicate. This is a pointed question. The answer should be specific and defensible.

**Why now: explicit about changes.** Techstars wants to know what changed. New technology, new law, new policy, new market conditions. Be specific about the trigger.

### Techstars competitive advantage framing

Techstars gives you explicit prompts to work with on the competitive advantage question:

- What makes you 10x better than competitors?
- What makes you 80% cheaper, 4x times faster?
- What counterintuitive approach are you using that is the right approach versus your competitors?
- What is hard for a competitor to replicate?
- Fill in the blank: "We are the only company in the world that _____ for our customers."

These are genuinely useful prompts. Use them to stress-test your answer.

### Milestones: next features or next learnings

Techstars asks about next milestones in a specific way: "What are your next features, or what are you learning to let you know you are on the right path?" This opens the door for learning-based milestones, not just feature launches. If you are at a stage where the next milestone is "validate that X segment wants this before building Y", say so.

### Techstars-specific advice that applies to any application

The Techstars guide emphasises two things worth borrowing:

1. **Share the draft with co-founders, mentors, and advisors before submitting.** This is always good advice.
2. **Progress over time, not just absolute numbers.** When sharing traction, show the trend, not just a single snapshot.

## Programs not covered

The guidance in `questions.md` applies to any program. If the founder is applying to PearX, Character Labs, Canopy, or another accelerator or pre-accelerator, use the general principles.

Do not invent program-specific patterns for programs not covered here. If a founder asks about a specific program not listed, say so and apply general principles.
