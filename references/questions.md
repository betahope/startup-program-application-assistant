# Question-by-question guide

This file covers the common questions that appear across startup program applications. Wording varies by program but the underlying question is usually the same.

For each question: what it is really asking, what good looks like, what to avoid, and before/after examples.

---

## Lead with your strongest signal

Before the specific questions, one cross-cutting point that matters everywhere. If you have a signal that carries weight (paying customers, a named partnership, a notable founder credential, an impressive achievement), put it in the first sentence of the answer it belongs to. Reviewers read fast and decide fast. Strong signals buried at the end of an application, or at the end of a long answer, get missed. The order in which you present information is itself a signal about what you think matters.

This is compatible with the inverted-pyramid guidance in `SKILL.md`: lead with the conclusion, follow with the proof. Applied to an answer with multiple pieces of information: lead with the strongest piece, then the rest.

## What is the problem you are solving?

**What it is really asking:** Who is struggling, with what, and why are existing solutions not good enough?

**What good looks like:**
- Specific to a defined group of people, not "everyone"
- Quantifies the pain where possible (cost, time, frequency, impact)
- Describes what those people currently do and why it fails them
- Does not describe the solution. Only the problem.

**What to avoid:**
- Writing the solution in the form of a problem ("people do not have an AI-powered X")
- Vague scale claims ("this is a huge problem")
- Making the problem sound bigger than it is by widening the target group

**Weak:** "People do not have an AI-powered mousetrap that automatically attracts and traps mice humanely."

**Strong:** "Mice droppings are the leading cause for restaurant owners in New York to receive a C or lower health grade from the health department. After a low-grade notice is posted, revenue drops by 80%, and 7 out of 10 restaurants close within 30 days."

## What is your company going to make to solve this problem?

**What it is really asking:** What is the product? What does it actually do? How does it work, mechanically?

**What good looks like:**
- Matter of fact description of what the product is
- Explains what it does AND how it does it, not just what it is
- A reader could roughly reproduce the product from your description
- Often works well with a comparison ("it is like X but for Y")

**What to avoid:**
- Marketing language ("revolutionary", "innovative", "disruptive", "next-generation")
- Vague category claims ("a platform for...")
- Stacking buzzwords (AI + blockchain + IoT + whatever)
- Describing what it could become rather than what it is

**Weak:** "Mousetrap AI is an innovative, disruptive technology that combines IoT, blockchain, and AI to provide the most advanced mousetrap in the market."

**Strong:** "Mousetrap AI uses cameras and sensors to identify a mouse and trigger the peanut butter bait. Once the mouse is trapped, we send you a text message and dispatch technicians to remove it from your restaurant."

**The variant trick (Paul Graham):** Describing your product as a variant of something the reader already knows is wonderfully efficient. "It is like Wikipedia but within an organization." "It is like an answering service but for email." "It is eBay for jobs." Do not worry that this makes your idea sound derivative. Some of the best ideas in history combined two existing ideas.

**The one-sentence test:** How close is the reader to reproducing your product after reading your description? If they cannot picture the product, the description has failed.

## Why is your team a winning team?

**What it is really asking:** What gives you an unfair advantage? Why are you the right people to solve this problem?

**What good looks like:**
- Specific achievements by specific founders
- A clear connection between the team's background and the problem being solved
- Evidence of seriousness (left jobs, built before, invested their own money, etc.)
- Something impressive about each founder, individually

**What to avoid:**
- Generic praise ("hard-working", "dedicated", "passionate")
- Listing the current startup as the "impressive achievement"
- Vague team descriptions that do not say who does what

**Weak:** "Jordan is an exceptionally dedicated person who gives 100% effort to every project he undertakes."

**Strong:** "Jordan built the payments infrastructure at Stripe from 2019 to 2022, where he led the team that processed $40B in annual transaction volume. Before that, he was the second engineer at a YC-backed company that was acquired by Square in 2018."

**The "most impressive achievement" question (YC-specific):** This is the most important question on the YC application. It is deliberately open-ended. It could be academic, technical, personal, or anything else. What matters is magnitude, not category. If nothing stands out, go with whatever you did that was hardest, preferably hardest intellectually. Internships at top companies count.

## What traction do you have?

**What it is really asking:** Is there evidence people want this? And is it progressing?

**What good looks like:**
- Specific numbers (users, revenue, growth rate, retention)
- Progress over time, not just absolute numbers
- Evidence of demand: customers, users, LOIs, waitlist, repeat usage
- Honest framing. If it is monthly revenue, say monthly. Do not pretend it is annual.

**What to avoid:**
- Pretending monthly revenue is annual (or vice versa)
- Absolute numbers with no time dimension ("we have 5,000 users" without saying when)
- Vanity metrics that do not reflect real engagement
- Extraordinary claims without extraordinary evidence

**Important:** No traction is OK at very early stages. Many accepted startups have none. Do not invent traction. If you do not have it, say so and focus on what evidence of demand you do have, even if that is qualitative.

**Willingness-to-pay conversations count as traction at the early stages.** A handful of qualified conversations where a buyer told you what they would pay, what they would not, and what would make them switch is a stronger signal than a long list of free signups. Name the people you spoke with where you can. "We spoke with the heads of operations at three regional hospital chains, all of whom said they would pay $X for Y once we can prove Z" beats "we did customer development."

## How do you make money, and how much could you make?

**What it is really asking:** How do you charge, and what is the ceiling on this business?

**What good looks like:**
- Clear pricing model (subscription, transaction, seat-based, per-project, etc.)
- A bottom-up TAM calculation (see below)
- Evidence that the market is big enough to support a venture-scale outcome

**How big is "big enough"?** For VC-backed programs, readers are looking for a path to roughly $100M+ in annual revenue or a potential billion-dollar outcome. The bottom-up TAM should show how the business could realistically reach that scale, not just assert it.

**What to avoid:**
- Top-down TAM numbers pulled from consultant reports ("the global X market is $X billion")
- Answering only "how we make money" without "how much we could make"
- Being unrealistic about conversion or capture rates

**Bottom-up TAM calculation (Techstars framework):**

1. Start with your customer. Who are you selling to? Be specific.
2. Figure out how much each customer pays you per year. This is your Annual Contract Value (ACV).
3. Estimate how many potential customers exist. Count real customers who fit your target.
4. Multiply. TAM = ACV × Number of Potential Customers

**Weak:** "We charge a monthly subscription of $100, and after year 3, we will reach break-even and reach $10M in annual revenue."

(This does not answer the second half of the question.)

**Strong:** "We charge a $1,200 annual subscription. With 749,404 restaurants in the US, we can reach $749M in annual revenue in the US. With 1.5 million restaurants in Europe, we can generate $1.8B per year in Europe."

## Who is your target audience?

**What it is really asking:** Who exactly is the person who has this problem, knows they have it, and will do something about it?

**What good looks like:** A specific persona that:
- Has the problem
- Knows they have the problem
- Has quantified the problem
- Will do something about the problem

The more specific, the better. This is your Ideal Customer Profile.

**What to avoid:**
- Overly broad targets ("small businesses", "companies that do hiring")
- Describing a market segment instead of a decision-maker
- Hand-waving about "everyone who needs X"

**Weak:** "Companies doing any type of hiring."

**Strong:** "Hiring Managers at US companies hiring 10 or more inside sales roles per quarter."

**Weak:** "Fitness centers."

**Strong:** "The 60 COOs of YMCA Metro Groups in the US, each controlling approximately 1,000 facilities that could pay $100K per facility for our solution."

## Who are your competitors?

**What it is really asking:** Do you know the landscape? Have you thought about who else is trying to solve this, including people who might not look like competitors?

**What good looks like:**
- Direct competitors (same solution, same target)
- Indirect competitors (substitutes, including doing nothing)
- Potential future competitors (large companies that could enter, or startups that could pivot in)
- Honest acknowledgement that competition exists

**What to avoid:**
- Claiming you have no competition (you always do; if nobody else is solving the problem, maybe there is no problem)
- Only listing direct competitors and ignoring substitutes
- Pretending ChatGPT, Excel, or "doing nothing" are not competitors when they are

**Including both large companies and new startups matters.** A program wants to see that you have considered the full landscape.

**Bonus signal: have you met the players?** Beyond listing competitors, having actually spoken with operators, founders, and analysts in the space is a strong signal that you have done the work. Reviewers can usually tell the difference between a competitor list assembled from a Google search and one assembled from real conversations. Where you have those conversations, name them.

## What is your competitive advantage?

**What it is really asking:** What makes you 10x better, 80% cheaper, or 4x faster than alternatives? What would be hard for a competitor to copy?

**What good looks like:**
- Specific, concrete differentiation
- Something that is defensible over time (a moat, not just a head start)
- Evidence or reasoning for why this advantage is real

**What to avoid:**
- "We are more user-friendly" or "better designed" (that is not an insight, it is a claim you will execute well, which everyone says)
- "We are more innovative" (meaningless)
- Vague claims of differentiation without substance

**Paul Graham's point:** Claiming your advantage is that you will build a better product is not an insight. The people who built the current bad product were presumably also trying to build a good one. So what exactly are you going to do differently, and why will it work?

**Useful framing questions:**
- What makes you 10x better than competitors?
- What makes you 80% cheaper, or 4x faster?
- What counterintuitive approach are you taking that is actually right?
- What is hard for a competitor to replicate?
- Why will you beat the natural monopolies in this space?
- Fill in the blank: "We are the only company in the world that _____ for our customers."

## Why is now the right time?

**What it is really asking:** What has changed in the world that makes this possible or necessary now, when it was not before?

**What good looks like:**
- A specific shift in technology, regulation, market behaviour, or cost structure
- A clear connection between the shift and why this startup can exist now
- Evidence of the shift (not just assertion)

**What to avoid:**
- Generic "AI is changing everything" framing
- Timing claims that do not connect to your specific business
- Claiming now is the right time without explaining why it was not five years ago

**Good prompts to answer this:**
- Are you solving a new problem that did not exist before?
- Are you solving an existing problem in a new way that was impossible before?
- What changed recently that made this viable?

## What is your customer acquisition strategy?

**What it is really asking:** How are you going to get to your first meaningful number of customers, and can you do it without burning infinite money?

**What good looks like:**
- A concrete path to the first 1,000 users or $2.5M in revenue
- Channels you can execute on, not theoretical ones
- Something you can do better, cheaper, or faster than a well-funded competitor could replicate
- Evidence you have already started (warm network, communities you are active in, early traction)

**What to avoid:**
- "We will do content marketing, SEO, paid ads, partnerships, and PR" (too broad, no focus)
- Channels that require large budgets you do not have
- Vague "we will go viral" claims
- Unrealistic conversion assumptions

---

## What are your next major company milestones?

**What it is really asking:** What are you working toward next, and what will tell you that you are on the right path?

**What good looks like:**
- Specific, time-bound goals
- A mix of product milestones and learning milestones
- Clear signals that will tell you if you are on track or need to adjust

**What to avoid:**
- Vague goals ("grow the business", "get more users")
- Only listing feature launches without outcomes
- Milestones that cannot be measured

---

## The YC wildcard: "Tell us about a time you most successfully hacked a non-computer system to your advantage"

**What it is really asking:** Are you the kind of person who figures things out and bends rules, rather than someone who just follows the system as given?

**What good looks like:**
- A specific, concrete story
- Something that actually required cleverness or determination
- Legal and ethical, but outside the normal path
- Shows character, not just outcome

**What to avoid:**
- Listing something boring that everyone does
- Stretching the truth
- Missing the spirit of the question (they are looking for people who beat the system, not follow it)

**Why this matters:** YC has invited people to interviews primarily on the strength of this answer. It is a genuine wildcard.

---

## Founder backstory: how did you meet, who does what?

**What it is really asking:** Is this a real team that will survive the grind? Do you know each other well? Does everyone have a clear role?

**What good looks like:**
- Specific history (we met at X, worked together on Y, have known each other for Z years)
- Clear role definition (who does what)
- Evidence of having worked together before, or having worked through conflict

**What to avoid:**
- "We are all passionate about the problem" without saying how you came together
- Vague roles ("we all do a bit of everything")
- Hiding the fact that you recently met (programs would rather hear the truth)

---

## Follow-up: if you have applied before and been rejected

**What it is really asking:** Have you learned? Are you more serious this time?

**What good looks like:**
- Acknowledge you applied before
- Briefly note what you have done since (more traction, better team, pivoted to a better idea, learned from feedback)
- Do not be defensive about prior rejections. They are a positive signal, not a negative one.

**Why this matters:** Programs track applicants across cycles. Re-applying with evidence of progress is heavily weighted in your favour. Many accepted YC founders applied 3, 5, or 7 times.
